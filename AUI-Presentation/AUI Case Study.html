03 / Understanding the Space

Everyone has the features. No one
has the moments.

Pixel screens calls. iOS transcribes voicemails. Samsung translates. Truecaller blocks spam. The capability surface is crowded.

What's missing is the moment between people, the warmth of context before a call, the silent assist during it, the handoff afterwards when you can't write things down.

The gap is human, not technical.
Google · Pixel
Call Screen Hold For Me Live Caption
Strong at filtering. Silent on what to do after the call.
Apple · iOS
Live Voicemail Transcribe Focus Modes
Polished surfaces. AI hides itself, sometimes too well.
Samsung · One UI
Live Translate Bixby Text Call Summary
Capabilities deep, discovery shallow. Most users never find them.
Truecaller
Spam ID Reverse Lookup Block
Defends against strangers. Does nothing for the people you love.
The Gap
Where genuine human value is still unmet.
Context before. Hands during. Handoff after. The seams between people, not the model.

04 / How I Approached This

Three questions, asked in order.

Useful AI in a phone app is not a list of features, it's a sequence of decisions about who, where, and why.
I let the questions choose the screens, not the other way around.

01
Who & What They Feel

Who are the real users, and what do they actually feel?

Not personas. Specific moments, the second after the dial tone ends, the second the call drops.

02
Where It Breaks

Where does the experience genuinely break down today?

AI is the answer when the human friction is already legible. I went looking for the friction first.

03
Trust, Not Capability

What would make AI feel trustworthy here, not just functional?

Capability is table stakes. The design problem is the second thing, and it's almost never the model.

06 / The Insight
The real design challenge isn't building AI that can do more
It's building AI that people actually trust enough to let in

Capability gets people interested. Trust is what makes them stay. Every decision here was about building something people feel in control using, with clear moments to review, approve, and decide.

07 / Design Principles

Three rules I refused
to break.

Every screen in this case study can be checked against these. If it fails, it doesn't ship.

01

Earn it
before you do it.

The AI never acts without consent. It offers, asks, waits. Action is always one explicit tap away from intent, never a default.

02

Show your
work.

Whatever the AI understood is always visible and editable. Never a black box. The interpretation is the interface.

03

Plain language
for everyone.

If a 67-year-old and a 19-year-old can't both understand the same sentence, the design isn't done. Plain words are the universal access pattern.

09 / Concept 01 - Before the Call

A warm strip of context,
right where you'd look.

The Problem

You're about to dial someone you haven't spoken to in months. What was the last thing? Who said what? The phone is silent on the only thing you actually need.

The Solution

On the contact card, a single line surfaces, drawn from prior calls and shared apps with explicit permission. Two sentences, never more.

The Trust Mechanic

A small "How does this know?" link opens the source, every line is traceable to a moment you opted into.

Press · Hold to Ask

A silent second pair of hands.

The Problem

"Choose between being present and getting information." Switching apps mid-call is a small disaster: lost context, lost rapport, lost answer.

The Solution

One assistant button at the bottom edge. Press-and-hold to ask a single question, calendar, contacts, last email. The answer floats above the call as a small card. Release returns to the call.

The Trust Mechanic

Trust is designed into the gesture. A press is a request. A release is an exit. No background behavior, no hidden state - just a clear, controlled exchange.

13 / Edge Cases

Good design
names what
can go wrong.

Four moments where AI in a phone app most often fails users, and the design decision that addresses each.

01 · Privacy

"I don't want to be recorded."

On-device processing. Nothing leaves the phone unless you tap "send." A single toggle in settings, visible, plain English, default off on first install.

02 · Wrong

"The AI got it wrong."

Every item on the Understand screen is editable inline before any action runs. One tap to dismiss, one tap to fix. The error never compounds.

03 · Personal

"The call was personal."

When there's nothing actionable, the AI says so, quietly. No fake tasks, no padded summaries. Empty is a valid output.

04 · Attribution

"Someone else was on the call."

"You said" and "they said" are always two columns, never blended. If unclear who said what, the item is dropped, never guessed.

14 / KPIs & Success Metrics

Metrics tied to behavior,
not downloads or engagement time.

Trust isn't directly measurable, but it shows up in behavior: consent, edits, undo, and day-30 retention.
These are the first signals I'd instrument.

What I deliberately left out, and why.

Onboarding · Not Designed

My hypothesis: the best onboarding for this is the first call. The Ask itself is the tutorial. I'd validate before designing a separate intro flow.

group calls

Attribution gets harder with three voices. The "you said / they said" model needs a rethink; I'd ship 1:1 first and learn.

The First Test I'd Run

The exact wording of "Want my help with this call?" - A/B against three alternatives. The phrasing carries half the trust signal.

Before shipping "Always Allow"

Do people who opt in come back more? If not, that's a signal to look deeper: is it helping, or quietly hurting the experience?

16 / Why AUI

The reliability gap
is a design problem.

AUI is one of the few teams treating AI reliability as the actual product, not a feature to bolt on after a launch event. That framing is rare, and it's what makes the work feel worth doing well.

Designing at Vee taught me the delegation gap is rarely a model problem, it's the second the user is asked to hand something over. The model can be right; the moment can still fail. That's where design lives.

This assignment was my way of exploring that problem through AUI's lens - focusing on trust, control, and the moments where reliability is actually felt.
